# Case: DaVita: A Community First, A Company Second
**Tags:** #case-study #resolved

**Course:** Leading People
**Source:** DaVitaACommunityFirstACompanySecond.pdf

## 1. Core Dilemma

CEO Kent Thiry must determine how to integrate the unique 'Village' culture of DaVita into the newly merged HealthCare Partners (HCP), a physician-led organization with a different operational model. He faces the challenge of scaling a high-intensity, values-based culture across a more heterogeneous business without diluting the community-first identity that drove DaVita's clinical and financial turnaround.

## 2. Key Stakeholders & Incentives

- **Kent Thiry ('KT')** (CEO of DaVita and 'Mayor' of the Village): Maintaining the values-driven culture, achieving the vision of being the 'greatest healthcare company,' and maximizing shareholder value through successful merger integration.
- **Robert Margolis** (CEO of HealthCare Partners (HCP)): Finding a stable partner for growth in a consolidating healthcare market and preserving the clinical autonomy and patient-centric focus of his physician networks.
- **DaVita Teammates** (Clinicians, nurses, and technicians): Emotional fulfillment, community support (Village Network), and financial security through profit-sharing and benefits.
- **HCP Physicians and Staff** (Medical professionals in the acquired entity): Maintaining professional autonomy, high-quality patient care, and adapting to a new corporate identity without losing their physician-led heritage.
- **Medicare and Private Insurers** (Payors): Reducing the cost of care (shifting to bundled rates/ACOs) and improving clinical outcomes to lower long-term expenditures.
- **Patients** (Dialysis and primary care recipients): Survival, quality of life, and receiving compassionate, integrated medical treatment.

## 3. Financial Context & Constraints

DaVita's 2012 revenue was $7.53 billion with $641 million net income. The HCP merger cost $4.4 billion ($3.4 billion net of tax benefits). Labor hours represent ~40% of dialysis costs, with every 0.01 hour saved per treatment yielding $5 million in bottom-line profit. The stock price rose from $5.28 in 2000 to over $60 by 2012.
