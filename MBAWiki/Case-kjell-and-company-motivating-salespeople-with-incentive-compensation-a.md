# Case: Kjell and Company: Motivating Salespeople with Incentive Compensation (A)
**Tags:** #case-study #resolved

**Course:** Leading People
**Source:** KjellAndCompanyMotivatingSalespeopleWithIncentiveCompensationA.pdf

## 1. Core Dilemma

Thomas Keifer must decide whether to transition from monthly to daily sales quotas to prevent salespeople from losing motivation when they fall behind early in a month. He must also weigh the benefits of an experimental pilot against the firm's egalitarian 'one-for-all' culture, which favors simultaneous company-wide changes.

## 2. Key Stakeholders & Incentives

- **Thomas Keifer** (CEO of Kjell & Company): Aims to maximize sales force motivation and performance while maintaining the company's unique culture of fairness.
- **Joel Rönneman** (Head of Sales): Ensuring regional managers and salespeople are effective; previously a sales associate himself, valuing the internal career path.
- **Martin Knutson** (CIO): Responsible for the technical implementation and data tracking of any new compensation structure.
- **Salespeople** (In-store staff (approx. 350)): Motivated by a mix of fixed salary and variable commissions; sensitive to quota frequency, stress, and perceived fairness of HR policies.

## 3. Financial Context & Constraints

In 2014, Kjell recorded revenue of 1.04 billion SEK (~$107M) with a 10% net profit margin and 17% market share. Salespeople receive a fixed salary averaging SEK 21,000 (~$2,300) plus a 5-tier commission based on Sales Per Hour (SPH), ranging from 0.27% to 2.0%.

## 4. Class Discussion & Takeaways

The Kjell and Company case was used to illustrate the importance of experimental design over simple 'all-in' rollouts. The company considered switching from monthly to daily quotas to keep salespeople motivated throughout the month. The discussion centered on whether the company should experiment first or implement the change globally, ultimately showing that an experiment revealed the change only slightly improved sales and actually had a negative impact on high performers.

### Key Takeaways

- Without a control group, you might mistakenly attribute a general sales trend (e.g., seasonal increase) to a policy change.
- Treatment effect heterogeneity: A policy that motivates low performers might demotivate high performers if it feels like a pay cut or unnecessary pressure.
- Experimental contamination can occur if employees in the control group discuss the new policy with those in the treatment group.

### Student Perspectives

- Some students argued for an 'all-in' rollout to ensure fairness and avoid resentment between stores.
- An 'opt-in' structure was proposed as a way to solve the resentment issue, though the professor noted this introduces self-selection bias.
- Concerns were raised about whether the initial problem (employees giving up) was truly widespread or just anecdotal.

### Professor Insights

- The professor used the case to demonstrate why randomization is superior to selecting 'similar' stores for a control group.
- Highlighted the 'Hawthorne effect' where the mere act of being studied can change employee behavior regardless of the treatment.
- Shared the quote from former Caesars CEO Gary Loveman: 'Running an experiment without a control group' is one of the three things that can get you fired.
