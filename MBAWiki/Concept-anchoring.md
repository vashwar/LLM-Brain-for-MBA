# Anchoring

**Course:** Leading People

## Definition

Anchoring is a cognitive bias where an individual relies too heavily on an initial piece of information (the "anchor") when making subsequent judgments or decisions, even if that anchor is irrelevant or arbitrary.

## Key Points

- **Insufficient Adjustment:** People start at the anchor and move away, but they usually stop too close to the starting point.
- **Selective Accessibility:** The anchor makes information consistent with that value more accessible in the mind.
- **Arbitrariness:** Anchoring occurs even if the anchor is completely meaningless (e.g., using random numbers).
- **Emotional Anchors:** First impressions can act as emotional anchors for all future interactions with a person.
- **Mitigation:** To mitigate anchoring, one should consider information opposite to the anchor or set a specific target/reservation price before hearing an offer.

## Examples

- **Negotiations:** First offers in salary or real estate act as powerful anchors that set the floor or ceiling for all subsequent counter-offers.
- **Price Estimates:** Asking if a German car costs more than $30k vs. $80k significantly biases the final price estimate given by respondents.
- **Arbitrary Numbers:** Using the last three digits of a phone number to estimate the year of a historical event.
