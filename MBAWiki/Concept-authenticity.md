# Authenticity

**Course:** Leading People
**Source:** LP_2024_class_3_WE.pdf, TheAuthenticityParadox (1).pdf, Fall 2024 - EWMBA 205-LEC-31A _ 32A - Leading People 8_17_24_Captions_English (United States).txt

## Definition

Authenticity is the degree of alignment between an individual's inner self (traits, beliefs, values, and emotions) and their outward expression (words and actions) in the workplace. It is closely linked to the **Authenticity Paradox**, which suggests that a rigid adherence to one's past or comfortable self-image can hinder growth and leadership development.

## Key Points

- **Dual Perceptions:** Authenticity can be self-perceived (feeling true to oneself) or other-evaluated (how authentic colleagues perceive the individual to be). There is often a weak correlation between these two.
- **Leadership Impact:** Other-perceived authenticity is critical for stakeholder trust and perceived leadership effectiveness. Self-perceived authenticity is linked to higher personal well-being, work engagement, and self-esteem.
- **The Paradox as an Anchor:** A rigid "true-to-self" identity can prevent leaders from adapting to new roles. Professional development often requires behaviors that feel "fake" or "unnatural" initially during the "stretching" process.
- **Inclusivity Challenges:** Minority groups often report feeling less authentic at work due to systemic pressures to conform to majority norms and dominant cultural expectations.
- **Pressure-Induced Regression:** Under stress, leaders often retreat to familiar behaviors that feel "authentic" but may be ineffective for their current role's requirements.

## Examples

- **Cynthia:** A manager who values transparency admits she is "scared" to her new team. While internally authentic, it undermines her creditability and [[Authority vs. Approachability]] balance.
- **George:** An executive valuing humility feels like a fake when forced to aggressively promote his ideas in a high-visibility culture.
- **Technical Experts:** An expert may feel inauthentic adopting the social or political behaviors required of senior executives, viewing them as "playing games."

The concept of the Authenticity Paradox was developed by Herminia Ibarra. Action and experimentation are often better paths to finding a "true" leadership self than pure introspection.
