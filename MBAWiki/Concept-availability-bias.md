# Availability Bias

**Course:** Leading People

## Definition

Availability Bias is a cognitive heuristic where individuals estimate the frequency or probability of an event based on how easily instances of it can be recalled from memory, rather than on more representative statistical data.

## Key Points

- **Ease of Recall:** Influenced by vividness and emotional intensity. Vivid, striking events (like homicides) feel more frequent than mundane ones (like accidental falls).
- **Retrievability:** Influenced by memory structures. For example, it is easier to think of words starting with "A" than words where "A" is the third letter, leading to overestimation of the former.
- **Recency:** Recent events are more available in memory and are weighted more heavily in current judgment.
- **Workplace Impact:** Significantly impacts performance appraisals, where managers often over-value an employee's most recent behavior rather than their performance throughout the entire year.

## Examples

- Overestimating the frequency of plane crashes because they are vividly reported, while underestimating deaths from pneumonia.
- A manager giving a poor annual review to a top performer because of one mistake made the week before the evaluation.
- People purchasing earthquake insurance immediately after an earthquake, even though the statistical risk has not increased.

## Notes

Vividness often trumps statistical frequency in human judgment. Breaking the bias requires looking at long-term data rather than relying on memory.
