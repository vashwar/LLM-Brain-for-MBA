# Balance Sheet

## Definition

A financial statement that summarizes a company's assets, liabilities, and [[Shareholders' Equity]] at a specific point in time, providing a snapshot of the company's financial position and liquidity.

## Key Points

- Follows the fundamental accounting equation: Assets = Liabilities + [[Shareholders' Equity]].
- Must remain balanced: transactions must impact at least two accounts to maintain equilibrium.
- Reflects cumulative amounts for all account balances.
- Assets must be categorized into current (liquid within a year) and long-term (non-current).
- Lists liabilities in order of maturity.
- Includes intangible assets like goodwill.
- Reflects historical acquisition costs for most assets rather than current market value.
- Provides a view of the company's financial health and liquidity position.

## Formulas & Equations

- Total Assets = Total Liabilities + [[Shareholders' Equity]]
- Net Worth = Assets - Liabilities

## Examples

- Applied Materials Consolidated Balance Sheet as of October 29, 2017.
- Netflix's asset classification.
- Recording an inventory purchase as a decrease in cash and an increase in noncash assets.
- Borrowing cash increases both assets and liabilities.
- Accounts Receivable, Inventory, Property, Plant and Equipment, Goodwill, Retained Earnings.
- Reporting cash, inventory, and PPE as assets.
- Listing accounts payable and long-term debt as liabilities.

## Notes

Assets are generally carried at historical acquisition cost. This is the foundation of double-entry bookkeeping. Includes illustrative accounts like [[Deferred Revenues]] and [[Accrued Liabilities]]. Always specify the date as it is a point-in-time statement.
