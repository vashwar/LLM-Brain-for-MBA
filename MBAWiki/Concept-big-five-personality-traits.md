## Definition

The Big Five Personality Traits (also known as the Five-Factor Model) is the most scientifically validated framework for personality, assessing individuals across five stable dimensions: Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, and Emotional Stability (Neuroticism).

## Key Points

- **The Dimensions:**
    - **Openness to Experience:** Intellectual curiosity and imagination.
    - **Conscientiousness:** Responsibility, organization, and dependability.
    - **Extraversion:** Sociability, assertiveness, and energy.
    - **Agreeableness:** Cooperation, trust, and empathy.
    - **Emotional Stability (Neuroticism):** Calmness vs. nervousness, anxiety, or insecurity.
- **Measurement:** Unlike type-based tests (like MBTI), it provides a score on a distribution for each dimension rather than fixed categories.
- **Workplace Predictors:** Extraversion and Conscientiousness are the traits most predictive of workplace success and leadership effectiveness.
- **Stability:** The model is considered more scientifically robust than MBTI and traits remain relatively stable in the short run.

## Examples

- **Predicting Performance:** High conscientiousness is a strong predictor of overall job performance, while high extroversion often predicts success in leadership and sales roles.
- **Illustrative Characters:** Fictional characters are often used to illustrate dimensions, such as Walter White (Breaking Bad) representing extreme conscientiousness or Rachel (Friends) illustrating agreeableness.
- **Organizational Use:** Firms may use the Big Five to assemble complementary teams or screen candidates for extreme emotional instability.
