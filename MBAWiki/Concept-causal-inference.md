# Causal Inference

**Course:** Leading People

**Source:** LP_2024_class_2_WE.pdf, Fall 2024 - EWMBA 205-LEC-31A _ 32A - Leading People 8_10_24_Captions_English (United States).txt

## Definition

The process of determining if a specific change in an independent variable (X) causes a specific change in a dependent variable (Y), establishing the actual effect of an intervention as opposed to mere correlation.

## Key Points

- The primary goal is to understand the cause-and-effect relationship ("why" something happens) rather than just forecasting outcomes (prediction).
- **The Fundamental Problem of Causal Inference:** It is impossible to observe the same unit in both the treated and control states at the exact same time.
- **Randomization:** Recognized as the gold standard for establishing causality because it ensures that the treatment and control groups are similar on all other factors, isolating the effect of the intervention.
- Calculated by comparing the Average Causal Treatment Effect across a sample.

## Formulas & Equations

- Yt,T - Yt,C = Causal Effect (The difference between the outcome for a unit under the treatment condition vs. the outcome for that same unit under the control condition).

## Examples

- Comparing the sales performance of a store that received a new commission structure vs. what its performance would have been under the old structure (the counterfactual).
- Using an experiment to see if a specific management training program actually causes an increase in team productivity, rather than just being correlated with it.

## Notes

In practice, researchers compute an average causal treatment effect by studying a sample of multiple subjects with random assignment to overcome the inability to see the counterfactual for a single individual.
