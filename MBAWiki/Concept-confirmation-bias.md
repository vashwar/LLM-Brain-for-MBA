# Confirmation Bias

**Course:** Leading People
**Source:** LP_2024_class_4_WE-1.pdf, Chapter3CommonBiases.pdf, Fall 2024 - EWMBA 205-LEC-31A _ 32A - Leading People 8_17_24_Captions_English (United States).txt

## Definition

Confirmation Bias is the tendency to search for, interpret, favor, and recall information that confirms one's pre-existing beliefs or hypotheses while failing to seek out or actively overlooking disconfirmatory evidence.

## Key Points

- **The "Confirmation Trap":** Individuals ask "May I believe it?" for supporting data and "Must I believe it?" for opposing data, making it difficult to falsify incorrect intuitive ideas.
- **Narrow Attention:** Leads to a narrow focus that can cause poor decisions in M&A, hiring, and project management.
- **Process vs. Outcome:** Bias is often reinforced when organizations reward final outcomes rather than the quality of the decision-making process itself.
- **Mitigation:** Can be reduced by actively seeking disconfirmatory information and institutionalizing dissent (e.g., assigning a "devil's advocate").

## Examples

- **Wason 2-4-6 Task:** People only test sequences that fit their suspected rule rather than trying to disprove it.
- **Wason Card Task (A, K, 4, 7):** When testing the rule "If a card has a vowel on one side, it must have an even number on the other," people frequently fail to flip the '7' card, which is the only card that could potentially disprove the rule.
- **Mergers & Acquisitions:** Leaders often only look for synergy data that supports the deal while ignoring red flags or cultural misalignments.
- **Investment:** Investors reading only bullish reports about a stock they recently purchased while ignoring bearish analysis.
