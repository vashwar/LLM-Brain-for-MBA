# Conjunction Fallacy

**Course:** Leading People
**Source:** Chapter3CommonBiases.pdf, Fall 2024 - EWMBA 205-LEC-31A _ 32A - Leading People 8_17_24_Captions_English (United States).txt

## Definition

The Conjunction Fallacy is a specific logical error where individuals judge a conjunction of two events (a subset) to be more probable than one of the constituent events in isolation.

## Key Points

- **Heuristic Origin:** It is a subset of [[Representativeness Bias]]; the conjunction "feels" more like the description than the general category does.
- **Probability Law:** Violates the mathematical rule that a subset (A ∩ B) can never be more probable than the set containing it (A).
- **Storytelling Trigger:** Vivid descriptions trigger mental storytelling that bypasses statistical reasoning, making the specific scenario seem more plausible.

## Formulas & Equations

- P(A ∩ B) ≤ P(A)

## Examples

- **The "Linda Problem":** People think Linda is more likely to be a "Bank Clerk and a Feminist" than just a "Bank Clerk" because the former fits the description provided better.
- **Natural Disasters:** Estimating a flood caused by an earthquake in California as more likely than a flood occurring anywhere in North America.
