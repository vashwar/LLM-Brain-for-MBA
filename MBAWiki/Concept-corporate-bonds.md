# Corporate Bonds

**Course:** Financial Accounting
**Source:** Session 06.pdf, Spring 2025 - EWMBA 202-LEC-31A _ 32A - Financial Accounting (Spring 2025)_Captions_English (United States) (3).txt

## Definition

A debt security issued by a company to raise large sums of capital from the public, requiring the issuer to pay interest (coupons) and return the principal at maturity.

## Key Points

- Bonds consist of a face value, coupon rate, and maturity date.
- The effective interest rate (market rate) is determined by the borrower's credit risk.
- Bonds can be issued at par, a discount, or a premium depending on the relationship between the coupon rate and the market interest rate.
- Par bonds sell at face value when coupon rate equals market rate; discount bonds sell below face value; premium bonds sell above face value.
- Interest expense is based on the effective interest rate, not the coupon rate.
- Zero coupon bonds pay no periodic interest, meaning the entire return is the difference between purchase price and maturity value.

## Formulas & Equations

- Coupon Payment = Coupon Rate * Face Value
- Interest Expense = Beginning Book Value * Effective Interest Rate
- Bond Price = PV of future coupon payments + PV of face value repayment

## Examples

- Zero coupon bonds
- Investment grade bonds
- High-yield (junk) bonds
- US Treasury securities
- Corporate bonds issued by Union Pacific

## Notes

The bond's book value must reach its face value at the time of maturity regardless of whether it was issued at a premium or discount. Discussion highlighted that bond jargon (coupons, par, etc.) often obscures the underlying economic reality of the loan.
