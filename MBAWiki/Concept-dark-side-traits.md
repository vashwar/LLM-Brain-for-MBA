# Dark Side Traits

**Course:** Leading People

## Definition

Dark Side Traits (often referred to as "derailers") are personality characteristics and qualities that are typically hidden but emerge under stress, pressure, or heavy workloads, potentially derailing a person's career. When taken to the extreme, these traits resemble common personality disorders.

## Key Points

- **Measurement:** These traits are measured by the Hogan Development Survey (HDS), identified by psychologists Robert and Joyce Hogan.
- **Prevalence:** Most people display at least three of these traits, with roughly 40% of individuals scoring high enough in at least one category to be at risk for career disruption.
- **Triggers:** Traits surface when cognitive resources are taxed (high stress) or when an individual is too relaxed and fails to exert self-control/reputation management (low pressure).
- **Categorization:**
    - **Moving Away ([[Distancing Traits]]):** Behaviors used to manage anxiety by distancing oneself from others (e.g., Excitable, Skeptical).
    - **Moving Against ([[Seductive Traits]]):** Behaviors that involve manipulating or charming others to get ahead (e.g., Bold, Mischievous).
    - **Moving Toward ([[Ingratiating Traits]]):** Behaviors focused on building alliances and seeking approval (e.g., Diligent, Dutiful).
- **Impact:** They negatively affect others' perceptions of leadership and act as barriers to career progression if not managed through self-awareness and active reputation management.

## Examples

- A leader becoming micromanaging or overly aggressive when facing a tight deadline.
- An "Excitable" leader who shows passion initially but becomes volatile and unpredictable under pressure.
- A "Diligent" manager who is meticulous in normal conditions but morphs into an obsessive micromanager under stress.

## Notes

Based on the work of Thomas Chamorro-Premuzic. Managing these traits is not about eliminating the underlying personality but about moderating and optimizing behaviors through self-awareness and self-regulation.
