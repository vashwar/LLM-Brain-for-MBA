## Definition

The systematic allocation of the cost of a long-term tangible capital asset (PPE) or intangible asset over its estimated useful life, treating the expenditure as an expense in the income statement rather than a one-time capital cost.

## Key Points

- Initial purchase of a capital asset is recorded as an increase in Property, Plant, and Equipment (PPE).
- Represents the prorated cost of using infrastructure assets in operations.
- Reduces the carrying value of assets on the [[Balance Sheet]].
- A non-cash expense added back when calculating cash flows from operations.
- Does not reflect changes in market value; it is a cost allocation mechanism.
- Amortization is the equivalent term for intangible assets.
- Requires management estimates (useful life, salvage value) which affect reported earnings.
- Land is not depreciated.
- GAAP typically uses straight-line depreciation, while tax rules may allow accelerated methods.

## Formulas & Equations

- Annual Depreciation = Cost / Useful Life
- Periodic Depreciation = (Cost of Asset - Salvage Value) / Useful Life
- Average Useful Life = Average Cost of Depreciable Assets / Annual Depreciation

## Examples

- Purchasing machinery for $5,000 and expensing $1,000 per year over five years.
- Prorating a $6,000 office computer machine over its 3-year useful life at $500 per quarter.
- Allocating factory building costs over 10 years.
- Spreading the production cost of a movie over three or four years.
- Home Depot's depreciation of stores and equipment.
- Google extending server useful life.

## Notes

Reflects the consumption of capital assets and ensures costs are matched against the revenue they generate. Crucial for calculating the net value of assets on the balance sheet. Changes in estimates are handled prospectively.
