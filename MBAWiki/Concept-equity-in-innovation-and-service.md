# Equity in Innovation and Service

**Course:** Leading People
**Source:** LP_2024_class_8_WE.pdf, TheyBoughtInNowTheyWantToBailOutHBRCaseStudy (1).pdf

## Definition

The degree to which an organization's products, services, and innovations provide equal value and quality to diverse customer segments. This often requires the effective use of [[Power in Organizations]] and [[Persuasion]] to overcome internal resistance when implementing new service initiatives.

## Key Points

- Service quality may unintentionally vary based on customer demographics.
- Innovations can embed societal biases if the development process is not inclusive.
- Technological solutions (like facial recognition) often demonstrate lower accuracy for marginalized groups.
- Informal settings (e.g., "outdoor meetings" or golf outings) are often used for high-stakes persuasion to gain support for new initiatives.
- Securing a "seed fund" is a critical initial step for innovation that requires persuading peers to contribute from their own budgets.
- A leader's ability to "nip complaints in the bud" and regain control of a meeting is essential for maintaining project momentum and ensuring service standards are met.

## Examples

- **Hospitality Platforms:** Service quality disparities observed on platforms like Airbnb.
- **Algorithmic Bias:** Facial recognition algorithms that struggle to correctly identify individuals with darker skin tones.
- **CRM Initiative:** Barry Golding pitching the CRM idea to CEO Howard Kempleman during a golf game to get a "provisional thumbs-up" before managing resistant department heads during a formal review.
