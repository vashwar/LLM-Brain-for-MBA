# Evidence-Based Management

**Course:** Leading People

## Definition

The practice of making organizational decisions and management choices based on the best available scientific research, data analytics, and rigorous experimentation. This approach emphasizes transforming raw data into causal insights and moving beyond intuition or "common sense," which scientific data often proves false.

## Key Points

- Distinguishes between raw data and convincing evidence; data from sources like the World Management Survey show strong correlations between management quality and firm productivity, profit, and growth.
- Management quality is often measured across specific dimensions including target setting, monitoring, and incentives.
- Requires understanding the causal relationship between an intervention (X) and an outcome (Y) rather than relying on simple observation or prediction.
- Advocates for the use of control groups and experimentation to validate business strategies and challenge common-sense myths.
- Scientific data often debunks common assumptions, such as the idea that brainstorming is always the best way to generate ideas or that money is the only factor that motivates employees.
- Identifies performance drivers, such as how a lack of incentive differentiation can cause high performers to regress to average performance levels.
- Employs survey data and "big data" to reveal truths about effective management and the adoption of practices across firms.

## Examples

- Kjell and Company testing a move from monthly to daily sales quotas using a control group of five stores.
- Caesars Entertainment requiring a control group for every experiment conducted.
- Using the World Management Survey to benchmark an organization's performance against industry standards regarding management practices.
- Identifying that failure to differentiate rewards for high vs. low performers often leads to a decline in overall talent quality.
- Testing whether brainstorming is actually an effective strategy for generating high-quality ideas compared to individual ideation.

## Notes

Evidence is distinct from prediction; prediction seeks to forecast outcomes while evidence seeks to understand cause-and-effect. Management research has increasingly moved from anecdotal evidence to empirical research (LP_2024_class_1_WE.pdf, slides 25 and 31).
