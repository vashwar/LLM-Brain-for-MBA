# Expectancy Theory

**Course:** Leading People
**Source:** LP_2024_class_5_WE.pdf, Fall 2024 - EWMBA 205-LEC-31A _ 32A - Leading People 8_24_24_Captions_English (United States).txt

## Definition

A motivation theory developed by Victor Vroom proposing that an individual's motivation is determined by the combined strength of three mental components: Expectancy, Instrumentality, and Valence. It suggests that motivation is a multiplicative product of these three factors.

## Key Points

- **Expectancy (E):** The belief that effort will lead to performance (Effort → Performance). It answers the question: "Can I do the task?" If the goal is perceived as impossible, expectancy is zero.
- **Instrumentality (I):** The belief that performance will lead to a specific outcome or reward (Performance → Outcome). It answers: "What happens if I do it?" and "Does achieving the goal actually result in the promised reward?"
- **Valence (V):** The value or importance an individual places on the outcome or reward (Outcome → Reward/Value). It answers: "Will I like the result?" or "Do I actually care about this reward?"
- **Multiplicative Nature:** Motivation = E x I x V. Because the factors are multiplied, if any single component is zero, the overall motivation will be zero, regardless of how high the other two factors are.

## Formulas & Equations

- Motivation = Expectancy x Instrumentality x Valence

## Examples

- **The $1 Million Textbook Challenge:** A student might not be motivated to memorize a 1,000-page textbook in 24 hours for $1 million. Even though they highly value the money (High Valence) and trust the rewarder will pay (High Instrumentality), they believe the task is physically impossible (Zero Expectancy), resulting in zero motivation.

## Notes

Managers have significant control over these factors. They can improve Expectancy through training and feedback, strengthen Instrumentality by ensuring rewards are consistently delivered, and boost Valence by identifying rewards that employees actually value.
