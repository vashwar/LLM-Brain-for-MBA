## Definition

The structured framework for designing tests within an organization, such as Randomized Controlled Trials (RCTs), to manipulate independent variables and observe their effects on dependent variables to provide reliable evidence for decision-making.

## Key Points

- **Random Assignment:** The hallmark of experimentation, ensuring subjects have an equal chance of being in treatment or control groups, which helps avoid self-selection bias (opt-in bias).
- **Control Groups:** Essential to establish a baseline and account for external factors (e.g., seasonal trends or market shifts) that might otherwise be mistaken for the effect of the treatment.
- **Independent Variables:** The factor being manipulated or the specific intervention being tested (the treatment).
- **Dependent Variables:** The outcome being measured to test the hypothesis (e.g., sales, productivity, or employee satisfaction).
- **Heterogeneity of Treatment Effects:** Recognizes that the same intervention might affect different subgroups differently (e.g., a change might help low performers but negatively impact high performers).
- **Research Participants:** Requires careful selection of data sources and subjects to ensure the findings are valid for the intended population.

## Examples

- Randomly assigning retail stores to either a "daily quota" group or a "monthly quota" group to measure the impact on sales per hour (SPH) while keeping others as a control group.
- Randomizing which employees receive a new management intervention or digital tool to see if it improves job satisfaction or performance.
- Testing a new marketing message by showing it to a random subset of customers while the rest see the standard message.
