# Feedback Best Practices

**Course:** Leading People
**Source:** LP_2024_class_5_WE.pdf, Fall 2024 - EWMBA 205-LEC-31A _ 32A - Leading People 8_24_24_Captions_English (United States).txt

## Definition

A set of principles and guidelines for delivering effective assessments and information to employees about their performance to drive growth, maximize motivation, and ensure organizational alignment.

## Key Points

- **Frequency:** Feedback should be frequent and context-dependent rather than reserved for annual reviews. Regular feedback prevents "surprises" and keeps perspectives aligned.
- **Specificity:** Feedback must be specific and candid to be actionable. Generic praise or criticism prevents behavioral updates because the receiver does not know exactly what to change or repeat.
- **Future-orientation (Coaching):** Effective feedback focuses on future behavior (coaching) rather than dwelling purely on past mistakes (evaluation). This increases the likelihood that the feedback will be incorporated.
- **Framing:** Constructive feedback should be framed as a pathway to improvement. Givers often underestimate the receiver's desire for feedback, especially in high-consequence scenarios.
- **Setting and Timing:** Deliver constructive feedback in private. It is often more effective when given in the morning, as people tend to be more receptive to developmental input earlier in the day.

## Examples

- **Avoiding the "Feedback Sandwich":** The practice of wrapping negative feedback between two layers of positive feedback (positive-negative-positive) is often discouraged. It can lead to lack of clarity, as employees may focus only on the positive and miss the critical developmental area.
- **Dean Rich Lyons:** Describes feedback as a "reward" for the recipient.
