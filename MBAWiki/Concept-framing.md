# Framing

**Course:** Leading People, Financial Accounting
**Source:** LP_2024_class_4_WE-1.pdf, Fall 2024 - EWMBA 205-LEC-31A _ 32A - Leading People 8_17_24_Captions_English (United States).txt, Article03.pdf

## Definition

Framing is a cognitive bias and communication strategy involving the selection of language and word choice to influence how information is perceived by an audience, particularly based on whether options are presented as potential gains or losses.

## Key Points

- **Domain of Gains:** People are generally risk-averse when options are framed as gains (choosing the "sure thing").
- **Domain of Losses:** People are generally risk-seeking when options are framed as losses (choosing the gamble to avoid a certain loss).
- **Loss Aversion:** Losses are psychologically more painful than the benefits derived from equivalent gains; we feel the pain of losing $50 more than the joy of gaining $50.
- **Decision Impact:** Different phrasing of the same statistical outcome can lead to completely different organizational support levels.
- Companies use AI to test whether specific terminology, such as 'strong' vs 'solid', resonates better with market expectations.
- Ambiguity checks are performed to ensure that corporate messaging is understood consistently across different listener personas.
- AI can analyze the sentiment or 'harmonics' of statements to predict how they might be interpreted by analysts.

## Examples

- **Layoffs:** Framing a plan as "saving 2,000 jobs" (gain frame) typically receives more support than framing the same plan as "losing 4,000 jobs" (loss frame).
- **Gamification:** Choosing a certain gain of 50 points over a 50% chance of 150 points (risk-averse in gains).
- **Medical Decisions:** Choosing a 50% chance of losing 100 lives over a certain loss of 50 lives (risk-seeking in losses).
- Refining the word choice in earnings call prepared remarks to ensure a professional and accurate tone.
- Using AI to gut-check if prepared remarks align with the brand's intended messaging.
