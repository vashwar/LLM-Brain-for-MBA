# Group Decision-Making under Uncertainty

## Definition

The collective process of evaluating options and reaching conclusions when information is incomplete, ambiguous, or distributed unequally among team members. Effective group decision-making requires mechanisms to aggregate diverse information and manage social biases.

## Key Points

- **Common Information Effect:** Teams are more likely to discuss and heavily weight information held by all members than unique information held by only one person.
- **[[Confirmation Bias]]:** Teams often overlook or suppress data that contradicts a preferred course of action or a preliminary conclusion.
- **Diversity:** Essential for introducing novelty and varied perspectives, but insufficient if the team lacks a culture where diverse voices are actually heard and integrated.
- **Independence:** To avoid "herd behavior" (revealed in the Asch experiments), group members should form individual thoughts or estimates before meeting collectively.
- **Decentralization:** Centralized hierarchies can bottleneck information; decentralized networks often produce more accurate collective estimates by leveraging the "wisdom of the crowd."
- **Equal Participation:** A key predictor of team accuracy. Conversations dominated by one or two members lead to lower collective performance.
- **Advocacy vs. Inquiry:** Teams should move from being "advocates" for a specific solution to "inquiring" about all available data to uncover hidden information.
- **Initial Public Votes:** Taking early votes can freeze the decision-making process and pressure members to conform to the early majority, suppressing critical unique information.
- **Data Integrity:** Quantitative calculations (G.I.G.O. - Garbage In, Garbage Out) are only as good as the underlying data quality; uncertainty regarding [[Data Integration and Purity]] can stall commitment.
- **Authority Presence:** The presence of a central authority figure (e.g., the CEO) significantly impacts a group's ability to resolve skepticism and commit resources.

## Formulas & Equations

- Expected Value (EV) = Sum of (Outcome Value × Probability of Outcome)

## Examples

- **The Grogan Air Simulation:** Unique positive information about a candidate ("Janet") was often missed because team members focused on shared negative information, demonstrating the common information effect.
- **Carter Racing Case:** Failure to analyze data from both successful and unsuccessful races led to a flawed decision-making process.
- **NASA Challenger Shuttle Disaster:** Suppressed safety data regarding O-ring failure in cold temperatures due to high-pressure environments and social conformity.
- **CRM Task Force Meeting:** Department heads refused to commit funding because the CTO could not prove the purity of data, and the CEO's absence weakened the ability to drive a unified decision.
