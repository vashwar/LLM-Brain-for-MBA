# Information Disclosure

**Course:** Financial Accounting
**Source:** Article08.pdf, Article14.pdf, Article09.pdf, Article06.pdf, Article03.pdf, Article02.pdf

## Definition
The regulatory or voluntary practice of requiring organizations to reveal specific internal data, such as financial or strategic information, to the public. It serves to reduce information asymmetry between firms and their stakeholders, particularly investors. The process involves reporting financial and non-financial data to regulators, investors, and the public. Transparency is a fundamental requirement for U.S. public markets to ensure accountability and enable informed decision-making.

## Key Points
- Enhances transparency for investor decision-making.
- Regulatory bodies often face political and industry lobbying pressure when setting disclosure requirements.
- Disclosure requirements may be adjusted to balance investor needs with the reporting burden placed on firms.
- FASB requires crypto assets to be disclosed on quarterly and annual statements.
- Disclosure aims to provide investors with relevant details while balancing security and proprietary concerns.
- Involves high compliance costs, especially for 'Scope 3' (supply chain) emissions.
- Often requires independent third-party verification or assurance to validate data.
- Increased disclosure obligations expose firms to heightened litigation and legal risks.
- FASB mandates for breaking out inventory purchases and employee compensation in financial statement footnotes.
- Compliance involves balancing the benefit of increased transparency against the significant costs of implementation and system redesign.
- Public companies must navigate risks regarding privacy and security when using AI tools.
- Uploading non-public, material information into public AI models poses a risk of unintended disclosure.
- IR departments must manage the tone and clarity of communications to ensure that key objectives are accurately conveyed to investors.
- Reporting actual financial results is distinct from providing forward-looking 'guidance' or estimates.
- Strategic goals should be communicated with metrics that reflect progress over appropriate time horizons rather than arbitrary quarterly intervals.

## Examples
- SEC requirements for public companies to report greenhouse-gas emissions.
- Reporting climate-related risks that have a material impact on a firm's bottom line.
- Disclosing crypto holdings separately from intangible assets like patents.
- Providing details on the nature and purpose of holding specific volatile assets.
- California laws requiring disclosure of greenhouse gas emissions.
- SEC climate disclosure rules.
- Voluntary ESG reporting.
- Quarterly disclosure of employee compensation, depreciation, and amortization in income statement footnotes.
- Annual disclosure of the reasoning behind the classification of selling expenses.
- Earnings releases and conference call scripts.
- Quarterly and annual financial reporting of actual performance.
- Disclosure of non-financial operational performance metrics.

## Notes
- The lecture highlights the tension between providing 'consistent and reliable' information to investors and the 'overly burdensome' nature of reporting complex data like Scope 3 emissions or sensitive security information.
- Companies are shifting from voluntary ESG reporting to rigorous, audited regulatory filings.
- The lecture highlights the tension between regulatory mandates for greater disclosure and corporate concerns over compliance costs.
- The article highlights how AI is being used to refine the disclosure process, but warns of the risks involved in data privacy.
- The authors argue that moving away from earnings guidance does not imply a reduction in overall transparency, but rather a change in the type of information prioritized.
