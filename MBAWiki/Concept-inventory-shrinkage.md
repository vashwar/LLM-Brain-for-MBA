# Inventory

**Course:** Financial Accounting
**Source:** Article11.pdf, Session 05.pdf

## Definition
Inventory represents assets held for sale in the ordinary course of business or goods to be consumed in the production of goods for sale. Inventory shrinkage is the difference between the recorded inventory value in a firm's books and the actual physical inventory on hand.

## Key Points
- Includes raw materials, work-in-process, and finished goods.
- Retailers value inventory at purchase price; manufacturers include raw materials, direct labor, and manufacturing overhead.
- Managed via cost flow assumptions like FIFO, LIFO, and Average Cost.
- Inventory is recorded on the [[Balance Sheet]].
- Shrinkage is caused by external theft (shoplifting), internal theft, and inaccurate record-keeping.
- It is often measured as a percentage of sales to normalize the impact of inflation.
- Retailers manage shrinkage by investing in security technology, surveillance, and store layout changes.

## Formulas & Equations
- Beginning Inventory + Purchases/Production - COGS = Ending Inventory
- Shrinkage as % of Sales = (Inventory Shrinkage Amount / Total Sales) * 100

## Examples
- 3M's inventory of raw materials and finished products.
- Target's retail inventory.
- A retailer records 100 units of an item but only finds 95 on the shelves due to undetected shoplifting.
- Home Depot using live-view parking lot cameras and locking up inventory to reduce shrinkage.
