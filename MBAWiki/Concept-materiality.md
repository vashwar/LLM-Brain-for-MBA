# Materiality

**Course:** Financial Accounting
**Source:** Article08.pdf, Article07.pdf, Article14.pdf, Article09.pdf, Article06.pdf

## Definition

A threshold used in accounting and regulation to determine whether information is significant enough to influence the economic decisions of a reasonable investor. In the context of sustainability, it often refers to how environmental, social, and governance (ESG) factors impact a company's value or how a company impacts the world, often referred to as 'double materiality'. Determining what information is relevant for investors is a core function of standard-setting boards like the FASB.

## Key Points

- Acts as a filter for reporting obligations (e.g., Scope 1 and 2 emissions are only reported if they are of material interest).
- Determines the scope of financial reporting regarding risks such as severe weather losses.
- Subject to interpretation and regulatory guidance.
- Changes in accounting estimates, such as asset useful life, must be evaluated for materiality.
- Companies must disclose and reconcile non-traditional metrics if they are material to understanding the bottom line.
- Regulatory bodies like the SEC mandate clear disclosure for significant accounting offsets.
- The FASB rejected certain disclosures on crypto, deeming them unnecessary for investor evaluation.
- The inclusion or exclusion of assets like NFTs or stablecoins is based on whether they meet the definition of enforceable rights.
- Companies like MicroStrategy argue that as assets become mainstream, specific disclosure requirements may lose relevance.
- Requires companies to assess which ESG metrics are significant enough to warrant audit and reporting.
- Double materiality encompasses both financial impact on the firm and the firm's impact on external stakeholders.
- FASB projects aim to improve the utility of financial statements by forcing the disclosure of previously aggregated data.
- Concerns regarding 'operability' and 'decision-useful information' highlight the debate over what constitutes material data for investors.
- The shift in reporting requirements reflects a move toward higher granularity in financial reporting.

## Examples

- Disclosing losses incurred due to severe weather events if they impact the firm's bottom line.
- Mandatory reporting of Scope 1 and 2 emissions when considered material.
- Alphabet disclosing the extension of server infrastructure lifespan as a material change impacting earnings per share.
- Excluding wrapped tokens and NFTs from specific fair-value accounting requirements.
- Declining to force disclosure of cryptographic private keys due to security and materiality concerns.
- Cigna assessing which ESG metrics are material enough to validate and audit.
- European Union rules requiring companies to report on double materiality.
- Requirement to disclose large expenses incurred by specific business divisions.
- Mandatory breakdown of employee compensation, inventory purchases, and intangible asset amortization.

## Notes

- Materiality is the central pivot point for the SEC's decision to mandate some disclosures while potentially omitting others that firms deem burdensome.
- The SEC emphasizes the necessity of reconciling pro forma figures with standard accounting figures.
- Concepts of materiality are used here to filter out 'unnecessary' details for investors.
- Companies struggle with the evolving definitions of materiality, particularly with international regulations versus domestic SEC standards.
- The FASB's decision-making process balances the burden of compliance against the goal of providing 'decision-useful information'.
