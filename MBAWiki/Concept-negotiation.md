# Negotiation

**Course:** Leading People
**Source:** LP_2024_class_9_WE.pdf, Chapter10MakingRationalDecisionsInNegotiations.pdf, Fall 2024 - EWMBA 205-LEC-31A _ 32A - Leading People 9_14_24_Captions_English (United States).txt

## Definition

A collaborative decision-making process by which two or more parties seek to create value and reach an agreement on how to allocate resources. It involves managing competitive and collaborative elements while avoiding psychological and strategic pitfalls. From a **decision-analytic approach**, it is a prescriptive framework focusing on how real-world actors behave to provide guidance to a focal negotiator based on the likely behavior of the opponent.

## Key Points

- **Nature of Negotiation:** Negotiation should be viewed as a partnership or collaboration to create value (the "pie"), not just a purely competitive struggle for resources or a zero-sum game.
- **Distributive vs. Integrative:** 
    - **Distributive:** Single-issue (usually price), competitive, "win-lose." More for one party means less for the other.
    - **Integrative:** Multiple issues (e.g., benefits, travel, promotion timeline), collaborative, and non-zero-sum. Adding issues to the table is the primary way to move from distributive to integrative negotiation via "logrolling."
- **Interests vs. Positions:** Effective negotiation requires understanding the "why" (interests) behind the "what" (positions).
- **Decision-Analytic Framework:** Prescriptive for the focal negotiator and descriptive for the opponent. It relies on identifying differences in interests, risk preferences, and time preferences.
- **Key Preparation Components:**
    - **[[BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement)]]:** The primary source of power. It is a situation (what you do if you walk away), not a specific number.
    - **[[Target Price]]:** An ambitious but plausible goal.
    - **[[Reservation Price]]:** The "walk-away" point or bottom line; this should never be shared with the other party.
- **Negotiation Pitfalls:**
    - **Winner’s Curse:** Settling for too little or feeling like you overpaid.
    - **Agreement Bias:** Accepting terms worse than your [[BATNA (Best Alternative to a Negotiated Agreement)]] just to avoid conflict or reach a deal.
    - **Hubris:** Walking away from a beneficial deal due to pride or ego.
    - **Dysfunctional Rivalry:** Focusing on "beating" the other side rather than optimizing your own absolute outcome.
- **Scoring Systems:** 
    - Quantifies non-numeric issues (e.g., assigning dollar values to vacation days) and weights them to evaluate multiple offers objectively.
    - Prevents focusing solely on the most quantifiable issue (like price) and allows for "apples to oranges" comparisons.
- **First Offers and Anchoring:** 
    - Making the first offer usually leads to better outcomes due to anchoring, provided the negotiator has an educated guess about the other party's [[Reservation Price]].

## Formulas & Equations

- **Total Utility (Scoring System):** Total Utility = $\sum (Weight\ of\ Issue_i \times Points\ for\ Option_i)$

## Examples

- **The Two Sisters and the Orange:** One sister wanted the juice, the other wanted the peel. Cutting it in half (distributive) was less efficient than giving each the specific part they valued (integrative).
- **Salary Negotiation:** A recruit and employer negotiating a package including salary, vacation days, start date, and signing bonus to expand the value pie.
- **Trade-offs:** Trading a lower purchase price for a faster payment schedule because the seller has a liquidity need.
- **Real Estate:** A seller listing a house at a high price to anchor the negotiation, even if market value is lower.

## Notes

Negotiation is framed as a process of value creation rather than just value division. Developed by Howard Raiffa, the decision-analytic approach serves as an alternative to pure game theory. Preparation and practice, including anticipating the other side's interests, are critical for success.
