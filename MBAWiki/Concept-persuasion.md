# Persuasion

**Course:** Leading People
**Source:** LP_2024_class_10_WE.pdf, PersuadingTheUnpersuadable.pdf

## Definition

The process of guiding others toward a desired outcome through communication, logic, and emotional appeals, focusing on winning agreement rather than forcing compliance. It involves navigating the psychological distinction between internal and external control to minimize resistance.

## Key Points

- **Importance of Buy-in:** Persuasion is essential for achieving long-term engagement and commitment, particularly in "grey" areas where there are no clear right or wrong answers.
- **Logic and Emotion:** Effective persuasion balances analytical reasoning with emotional appeals to resonate with the target.
- **Internal vs. External Control:** 
    - Individuals with "internal control" believe they determine their own outcomes and are highly resistant to external pressure.
    - Stubborn people typically favor internal control and react poorly to forceful arguments.
- **The "Snap Back" Effect:** Using forceful arguments on those with high internal control can cause them to "snap back," moving further away from the desired opinion to assert their autonomy.
- **Ownership and Inquiry:** Persuasion is more effective when the influencer asks questions (e.g., "What if?") that allow the target to take ownership of the idea and feel they are shaping the outcome.

## Examples

- **Hollywood Screenwriting:** Writers treating a pitch like a "game of catch" so executives feel they are actively shaping the story, thus increasing their commitment to the project.
- **The Apple TV Project:** Mike Bell getting Steve Jobs to support Apple TV by letting Jobs argue for the very points Bell had originally pitched, allowing Jobs to feel the idea was his own.
- **Organizational Change:** A leader convincing a team to adopt a new CRM system by highlighting its specific benefits to their workflow rather than mandating its use through authority.
