## Definition

A unit-free measure of how responsive consumers are to changes in the price of a product. It is calculated as the percent change in quantity demanded divided by the percent change in price.

## Key Points

- Measures consumer sensitivity to price changes.
- Unit-free calculation allows for comparison across different goods.
- Always negative due to the law of demand.
- Positive cross-price elasticity indicates goods are substitutes; negative cross-price elasticity indicates goods are complements.
- Can be measured via ratios or calculus.
- Determines the profit-maximizing price when combined with marginal cost.
- Central to understanding the law of demand.
- Elasticity is not constant along a linear demand curve; it varies based on price and quantity.
- A firm's pricing strategy must account for whether consumers are in an elastic or inelastic region of the demand curve.
- Revenue maximization occurs at the point of unit elasticity (absolute value of 1).

## Formulas & Equations

- E = (% change in Q) / (% change in P)
- Point Elasticity = (dQ/dP) * (P/Q)
- Cross-Price Elasticity = (% change in Q of Product A) / (% change in P of Product B)
- MR = P * (1 - 1/|E|)

## Examples

- Changes in quantity demanded when pizza prices change.
- Model 3 price elasticity of -3.636.
- Ford Escort elasticity of -6.1.
- Ben & Jerry's vs. Häagen-Dazs (substitutes).
- Ice cream vs. cones (complements).
- Gasoline often shows inelastic demand in the short run.
- Luxury brands like BMW typically exhibit lower elasticity than mass-market vehicles.
