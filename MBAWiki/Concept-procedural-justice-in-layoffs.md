# Procedural Justice in Layoffs

**Course:** Leading People
**Source:** LP_2024_class_7_WE.pdf, Fall 2024 - EWMBA 205-LEC-31A _ 32A - Leading People 9_7_24_Captions_English (United States).txt

## Definition

The principle that the perceived fairness of the methods, communications, and processes used to implement staff reductions or terminations is just as important as the outcome itself for maintaining organizational morale and individual well-being.

## Key Points

- People care as much about "how" a decision is made as "what" the outcome is; high procedural justice can mitigate the negative impact of a layoff.
- Unfair processes in negative outcomes lead to moral outrage and long-term reputational damage, whereas fair processes can lead to understanding even when the outcome is negative.
- Effective layoff communication requires a clear and honest rationale, empathy, compassion, and the direct involvement of the CEO.
- Managers must address the concerns of both the "victims" (those being fired) and the "survivors" (those remaining), as survivor morale depends on the perceived fairness of how their colleagues were treated.

## Examples

- **Amazon Case Study:** Analyzing Andy Jassy’s layoff communication letter, which was noted for its clinical tone and lack of empathy.
- **Stripe Case Study:** The Stripe CEO's layoff communication serves as a positive counter-example, utilizing empathy and a clear, honest rationale.
- **Process vs. Outcome:** The contrast between a fair process (disappointing but understanding) and an unfair process (moral outrage).
