# Psychological Safety

**Course:** Leading People
**Source:** LP_2024_class_6_WE-1.pdf, WhatIsPsychologicalSafety.pdf, Fall 2024 - EWMBA 205-LEC-31A _ 32A - Leading People 8_24_24_Captions_English (United States).txt

## Definition

A shared belief held by members of a team that the team is a safe environment for interpersonal risk-taking. It is a climate where individuals feel comfortable speaking up with ideas, questions, concerns, or mistakes without fear of punishment, humiliation, or retribution.

## Key Points

- **Team-Level Construct:** Psychological safety is a collective atmosphere (team-level climate) rather than an individual personality trait (like extraversion) or a dyadic relationship (like trust between two people).
- **Hierarchy Mitigation:** High perceived hierarchy often leads to silence. Leaders can foster safety by flattening perceived hierarchies and actively admitting their own fallibility to encourage others to do the same.
- **Learning from Failure:** By reducing the negative consequences of mistakes, psychological safety encourages learning behaviors such as seeking feedback, sharing information, and admitting errors.
- **Critical for Performance:** It is a foundational component for effective team decision-making and preventing catastrophic failures, particularly in high-stakes fields like medical surgery, aviation, and engineering.
- **Voice vs. Silence:** Without psychological safety, employees may stay silent on important issues (e.g., safety concerns or quality flaws) to protect themselves, even when they know a project is at risk.

## Examples

- **Healthcare:** Nursing teams where fear of hierarchy prevented junior members from asking questions during surgery, contributing to preventable medical errors. Conversely, teams that conduct open post-surgery reviews of mistakes show higher safety.
- **Aviation:** Boeing quality control issues where a culture of fear allegedly prevented engineers from reporting safety concerns.
- **Project Management:** A junior team member pointing out a potential flaw in a plan to a senior leader without fear of being labeled "uncooperative."
