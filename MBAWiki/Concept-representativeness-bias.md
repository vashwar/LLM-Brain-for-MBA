# Representativeness Bias

## Definition

Representativeness Bias is a heuristic where people judge the probability of an event by how much it resembles a stereotype, typical case, or category member, often while ignoring statistical base rates.

## Key Points

- **Base Rate Neglect:** Causes individuals to ignore the statistical prevalence of an event in favor of a vivid description.
- **Conjunction Fallacy:** Leads to the belief that a combination of events is more likely than a single general one because the combination "feels" more representative.
- **Storytelling vs. Stats:** Vivid descriptions trigger mental storytelling that bypasses statistical reasoning.
- **Misconception of Chance:** Believing small samples should perfectly resemble the general population.
- **Mitigation:** Can be mitigated by collecting better data and using [[Conditions for Collective Intelligence]] to challenge individual stereotypes.

## Formulas & Equations

- Explained through Bayes' Theorem logic: $P(A|B) = \frac{P(B|A) \cdot P(A)}{P(B)}$, where people often ignore the prior probability $P(A)$.

## Examples

- **The "Linda Problem":** Ranking "bank clerk and active in the feminist movement" as more likely than just "bank clerk."
- **Librarian vs. Salesman:** Assuming a quiet person is a librarian despite there being many more salespeople in the population (ignoring base rates).
- **Startup Appearance:** Assuming a startup with a certain look or founder profile will be successful because it resembles famous past successes like Airbnb.
