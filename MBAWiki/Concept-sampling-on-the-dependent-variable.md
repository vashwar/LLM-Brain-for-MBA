# Sampling on the Dependent Variable

## Definition

A logical error and form of selection bias where a decision-maker selects cases based on a specific outcome (e.g., only looking at "successes") while ignoring cases with the opposite outcome. This prevents the observer from seeing the full variation in data, making it impossible to determine true causality.

## Key Points

- **Variation Requirement:** To identify the causes of success, one must study both thriving and floundering cases. Without variation in the outcome, you cannot see if a specific feature is truly correlated with success.
- **Success Story Trap:** Business literature often generalizes from successful companies (e.g., Airbnb, Netflix). Traits like "persistence" might be present in both successes and failures; if both groups share the trait, the trait cannot be the cause of success.
- **Survival Bias:** Closely related to survival bias, where the data set only includes those who "survived" a process, hiding the characteristics of those who did not.
- **Causality:** Sampling only on the outcome makes it impossible to distinguish between a driver of performance and a common, irrelevant trait.

## Examples

- **Abraham Wald's WWII study:** Military personnel wanted to reinforce planes where they saw bullet holes. Wald realized they were "sampling on the survivors." The vulnerable areas were the spots where returning planes had *no* hits, because planes hit in those spots never returned.
- **The "Brushing Teeth" Fallacy:** Successful founders brush their teeth, but so do failed ones; therefore, brushing teeth is not a cause of startup success.
- **Entrepreneurship Research:** Studying only successful founders to see if they "ask insightful questions" without checking if failed founders did the same thing.
- **The Challenger Disaster:** Engineers failed to analyze data from successful launches at all temperatures, missing the correlation between cold and O-ring failure.
