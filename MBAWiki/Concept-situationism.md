# Situationism

**Course:** Leading People
**Source:** LP_2024_class_3_WE.pdf, CouldYourPersonalityDerailYourCareer.pdf, Fall 2024 - EWMBA 205-LEC-31A _ 32A - Leading People 8_17_24_Captions_English (United States).txt

## Definition

The psychological perspective that behavior and actions are primarily determined by an individual's immediate environment, external factors, surroundings, and professional roles. It suggests that context determines whether a particular personality trait will be advantageous or detrimental and that individuals may act differently depending on the situation.

## Key Points

- **Contrast with [[Dispositionism]]:** While dispositionism focuses on internal traits, situationism focuses on how external constraints and context shape behavior.
- **Role-Based Behavior:** Argues that specific professional roles within an organization heavily impact and reward certain behaviors (e.g., "Imaginative" traits in innovation vs. risk management).
- **Stress as a Trigger:** Stress is a primary situational trigger that taxes cognitive resources and brings "derailers" (dark-side traits) to the surface.
- **Low Monitoring:** Too little pressure or high familiarity can cause individuals to stop monitoring their behaviors, revealing their dark side as social etiquette thresholds lower.
- **Overriding Traits:** Situational constraints often override individual personality traits in formal settings.
- **Authenticity Intersection:** Situationism intersects with dispositionism at the concept of [[Authenticity]], as individuals navigate the tension between their "true self" and situational demands.

## Examples

- A person acting as an extrovert in a professional meeting or leadership role while being naturally introverted in private life.
- Government agencies (like Homeland Security) requiring employees to adapt their behavior and communication styles to the cultural preferences of foreigners.
- An "Imaginative" trait being highly beneficial for an entrepreneur but worrisome for someone in a compliance or risk management role.
- A leader who is on their "best behavior" in public work settings but shows their true dark-side traits in private settings where social pressure is reduced.
