# Statement of Cash Flows

**Course:** Financial Accounting

## Definition

A financial statement that explains the change in a company's cash balance between two periods by categorizing cash inflows and outflows into three buckets: operations, investing, and financing. It shows how changes in balance sheet accounts and income affect cash and cash equivalents.

## Key Points

- Provides insight into liquidity that the income statement may mask due to [[Accrual Accounting]].
- Explains how a firm generates and uses cash.
- Reconciles accrual-based net income to actual cash movements.
- Serves as a check on the quality of reported earnings (provides a sanity check).
- Required because accrual earnings do not match cash flows on a quarter-by-quarter basis.
- Can be prepared using the direct or indirect method.
- Cash flow from operations (CFO) is the cash counterpart to net earnings.
- Cash flow from investing (CFI) relates to CapEx and acquisitions.
- Cash flow from financing (CFF) tracks transactions with debt and equity holders.

## Formulas & Equations

- Cash from Operations = Net Income + Depreciation - Change in (noncash) Working Capital
- Free Cash Flow = Cash from Operations + Cash from Investing (Net CapEx)

## Examples

- Cash inflows from customers.
- Cash outflows for capital expenditures (CAPEX).
- Cash inflows from issuing stock.
- Dividends paid in fiscal 2017.
- Proceeds from debt or stock issuance.
- Home Depot's cash flow reconciliation.
- Rivian's early-stage cash burn.

## Notes

Necessary because accrual-based net income does not directly equate to cash availability. Companies use either direct or indirect methods; most use the indirect method to reconcile net income to cash.
