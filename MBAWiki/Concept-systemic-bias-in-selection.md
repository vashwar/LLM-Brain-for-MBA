# Systemic Bias in Selection

**Course:** Leading People
**Source:** LP_2024_class_8_WE.pdf, LP_2024_class_7_WE.pdf, SelectionBiasAndThePerilsOfBenchmarking.pdf

## Definition

Systemic and perceptual errors in judgment that occur during selection processes, including recruitment, hiring, and business benchmarking. These biases lead evaluators to favor certain candidates or companies based on demographic characteristics, irrelevant criteria, or the distortion caused by the fact that poorly performing entities often disappear from the sample.

## Key Points

- **Hiring Bias:** Selection processes often favor certain demographics (e.g., male over female) even when talent and qualifications are equal. Bias persists through recruitment, compensation, and promotion phases.
- **Similarity Error:** A form of [[Homophily]] where interviewers tend to favor candidates who are similar to them in terms of background, personality, or interests.
- **Halo Effect:** Allowing one positive trait (e.g., physical attractiveness or a prestigious university) to disproportionately color the overall perception of the candidate.
- **Selection Bias in Benchmarking:** Conclusions about business performance are often distorted because poorly performing firms tend to fail and disappear, leaving a sample of "best practices" that is not representative of the entire population.
- **Industry Susceptibility:** Managers in mature industries are more susceptible to benchmarking bias because the failure rate has already fallen off. Conversely, Venture Capitalists are less likely to suffer from this as they observe high failure rates (approx. 90%) firsthand.
- **Internal Selection Systems:** Current managers within a firm are more likely to have a history of success than failure, which can bias their perspective (e.g., managers at Coca-Cola or P&G looking at established brands).
- **Mitigation:** Techniques such as blind evaluations (removing names) or extending benchmarking to include less-than-successful firms can reduce these biases.

## Examples

- **Goldin & Rouse (2000):** A study showing that blind auditions (using a screen) significantly increased the likelihood of female musicians being hired by major orchestras.
- **Racial Disparities:** Empirical evidence showing significant disparities in hiring rates for Black musicians compared to white musicians despite similar talent levels.
- **The U.S. Tire Industry:** By 1936, only 49 firms survived out of 274 that existed in 1922; studying only the survivors ignores 80% of the original population and their practices.
- **Hobby Bias:** An interviewer giving a higher rating to a candidate who enjoys the same hobby, such as tennis, regardless of its relevance to the job.

## Notes

- Similarity error and the halo effect are primary reasons why unstructured interviews often fail to predict actual job performance (LP_2024_class_7_WE.pdf, Slide 30).
- Authors suggest that to truly understand the drivers of performance, managers must look at both thriving and floundering companies to mitigate survival bias.
