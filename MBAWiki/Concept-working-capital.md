## Definition

A measure of a company's operational efficiency and short-term financial health, calculated as the difference between current assets and current liabilities, representing the net liquid resources available for operations and the amount of capital tied up in operating activities.

## Key Points

- Changes in non-cash working capital must be adjusted for when calculating cash flows from operations.
- Increase in non-cash current assets (e.g., inventory, receivables) is negative for cash flow; increases in current liabilities (e.g., accounts payable) provide cash (are positive for cash flow).
- Reflects cash tied up in day-to-day operations.
- Management aims to optimize this via the cash conversion cycle.
- Shareholders generally prefer lower working capital to free up tied-up cash.

## Formulas & Equations

- Working Capital = Current Assets - Current Liabilities

## Examples

- Accounts Receivable, Inventory, Accounts Payable, [[Accrued Liabilities]].

## Notes

Monitoring working capital is essential for understanding short-term liquidity. Non-cash working capital change is a critical adjustment in the indirect cash flow method. Managing working capital efficiently can optimize a company's cash conversion cycle.
